1. Purpose

This Policy governs the lifecycle of every AI system Cloudax develops or operates, from concept through decommissioning. It implements the largest single Annex A objective in ISO/IEC 42001:2023 (A.6) and is the most operationally significant subordinate AI policy.

ISO 27001:2022 references: ISO 42001 reference: A.6.1.2 Objectives for responsible development of AI system; A.6.1.3 Processes for responsible design and development of AI systems; A.6.2.2 AI system requirements and specification; A.6.2.3 Documentation of AI system design and development; A.6.2.4 AI system verification and validation; A.6.2.5 AI system deployment; A.6.2.6 AI system operation and monitoring; A.6.2.7 AI system technical documentation; A.6.2.8 AI system event logs

2. Scope

This Policy applies to every AI system within the AIMS scope (AIMS-SCOPE-001), whether developed in-house or assembled from third-party components. It applies regardless of system size or risk band; the depth of activity at each stage scales with assessed impact (AISIA, AI-POL-004).

3. Definitions

  • AI lifecycle: the stages an AI system passes through from initial concept to decommissioning.
  • Verification: confirmation that the AI system has been built correctly against its specification.
  • Validation: confirmation that the AI system performs as intended in its operational context.
  • Drift: change over time in the relationship between the AI system’s inputs, behaviour, or outputs that affects performance or impact.

4. Principles

  • Every AI system has a defined lifecycle owner.
  • Every stage produces documented evidence — not just for audit but to support handover, debugging, and learning.
  • Verification and validation are distinct activities, both performed.
  • Production behaviour is monitored continuously, not just at deployment.
  • Decommissioning is treated as deliberately as deployment.

5. Roles and Responsibilities

  • Chief Technology Officer: owns this Policy; accountable for the AI lifecycle discipline across all in-scope systems.
  • AI Builders (engineering, ML): execute the lifecycle for the AI systems they own.
  • AI Governance Lead: ensures AISIA, monitoring, and documentation are present at the required stages; signs off lifecycle gates.
  • Privacy Lead: contributes input where personal data is processed in development or operation.

6. Objectives for Responsible Development

Cloudax’s objectives for responsible AI development:

  • AI systems serve the customer purpose for which they are designed; functions outside that purpose are not deployed.
  • AI systems perform within documented accuracy and behaviour bounds.
  • Bias is actively identified, measured, and mitigated; fairness is a development goal, not an afterthought.
  • AI behaviour is observable in production; outputs that look anomalous trigger investigation.
  • AI systems can be adjusted, disabled, or rolled back rapidly when needed.
  • AI dependencies (data, models, third-party providers) are documented and can be substituted with reasonable effort.

7. Lifecycle Stages

7.1 Concept and Requirements

  • Define the customer or business purpose.
  • Identify affected parties; trigger AISIA per AI-POL-004.
  • Define functional and non-functional requirements (Section 8).
  • Identify data needs and provenance (cross-reference to AI-POL-006).
  • Identify third-party AI dependencies (cross-reference to AI-POL-009).
  • Concept review by AI Governance Lead before significant development investment.

7.2 Design and Development

  • Document architectural decisions, model choices, data pipeline, evaluation strategy.
  • Apply secure development principles (cross-reference to POL-024).
  • Build with monitoring instrumentation from the start, not retrofitted.
  • Maintain reproducibility — model training and evaluation can be re-run from documented inputs.

7.3 Verification and Validation

  • Verify against the specification (“we built it right”).
  • Validate in operationally representative conditions (“we built the right thing”).
  • Evaluate accuracy across population segments where bias is a risk.
  • Evaluate adversarial robustness, prompt injection resistance, and edge-case behaviour.
  • Document evaluation methodology, datasets, and results.

7.4 Deployment

  • Deployment follows the change management discipline (POL-025 plus AI-PRO-005).
  • AISIA must be current and signed off before deployment.
  • Monitoring must be operational before customer traffic is routed.
  • Rollback path is tested before deployment.
  • Customer-facing documentation reflects the deployed capability.

7.5 Operation and Monitoring

  • Continuous monitoring of accuracy, drift, fairness indicators (where instrumented), and incident signals.
  • Periodic evaluation refresh — at least annually.
  • Operational logs preserved for the period required for investigation and learning (Section 13).
  • AISIA refresh on the cadence set by AI-POL-004.

7.6 Decommissioning

  • Customers using the AI system are notified per their contract.
  • Data associated with the system is handled per the Records Retention Policy (POL-009) and the AI Data and Models Register (AI-REG-DATA-001).
  • Models and weights are archived or deleted per the Information Deletion Procedure (PRO-017).
  • The AI System Inventory (AI-REG-INV-001) is updated to reflect decommissioning.
  • Lessons-learned review at decommissioning captures longitudinal observations.

8. Requirements and Specification

  • Each AI feature has a documented specification covering: intended use, intended user, intended environment, intended data inputs, intended outputs, performance requirements, fairness requirements, safety requirements, monitoring requirements.
  • Out-of-intended-use scenarios are explicitly identified and addressed by design or by guardrails.
  • Specifications are versioned and held with the AI system documentation.

9. Documentation of Design and Development

  • Design decisions are recorded with rationale and alternatives considered.
  • Model selection (in-house, fine-tuned, third-party foundation model) is documented with the trade-offs evaluated.
  • Training, evaluation, and fine-tuning pipelines are documented and version-controlled (Infrastructure-as-Code where feasible).
  • Documentation supports onboarding of new engineers and audit by external parties.

10. Verification and Validation

  • Verification confirms the AI system meets its specification — unit tests, integration tests, evaluation against held-out data.
  • Validation confirms the AI system performs as intended in operation — staged rollout, customer pilot where appropriate, monitoring of real-world signals.
  • V&V evidence is retained per POL-009.
  • V&V is repeated at material change.

11. Operation and Monitoring

  • Production AI systems are monitored continuously.
  • Monitoring covers: input distribution drift, output accuracy or quality, fairness indicators, anomaly detection, error rates.
  • Alerts are routed to the on-call rota with documented response procedures.
  • Where monitoring detects an issue, the response follows AI-PLAN-IRP-001 if it is or may be an incident.
  • Periodic monitoring summaries are reported to AIMS management review.

12. Technical Documentation

  • Technical documentation is maintained for each AI system, covering at minimum: purpose, capabilities, limitations, intended use, training data summary, model details, performance characteristics, monitoring, support.
  • Technical documentation supports customer questions, audit, and onboarding.
  • Where appropriate, customer-facing documentation is derived from technical documentation.
  • Technical documentation is updated alongside material AI system changes.

13. Event Logs

  • AI systems produce event logs covering: requests, inferences, decisions, errors, monitor signals, configuration changes, deployments.
  • Logs are retained per POL-009 and POL-019 (cross-reference).
  • Logs support: incident investigation, accuracy evaluation, fairness analysis, audit, customer support.
  • Personal data in logs is minimised, pseudonymised, and protected per POL-010.

14. Change Management

  • AI changes are managed under POL-025 Change Management Policy plus AI-PRO-005 (AI-specific change management).
  • Material AI changes (new model, new third-party provider, material accuracy or fairness change) trigger AISIA refresh and verification refresh.
  • Change records reference the affected AI system entry in AI-REG-INV-001.

15. Coordination with the ISMS

AI lifecycle activity inherits security disciplines from the ISMS — secure development (POL-024), change management (POL-025), configuration management (POL-015), logging (POL-019), monitoring (POL-020), backup (POL-018), cryptographic controls (POL-023). This Policy adds AI-specific concerns; it does not replace the security policies.

16. Records

  • AI system records are held in AI-REG-INV-001.
  • Lifecycle stage records (specifications, design decisions, V&V outputs, deployment records, monitoring summaries) are retained per POL-009.

17. Compliance Monitoring

  • Compliance is monitored under AI-PROG-COMP-001 and audited under AI-PROG-AUDIT-001.
  • Auditors will typically sample AI systems and trace each through the lifecycle stages.

18. Maintenance

This Policy is reviewed at least annually and on material change to AI development practice or regulatory expectations.

19. Acknowledgement

AI Builders, the CTO, and the AI Governance Lead acknowledge this Policy on commencement, on material change, and on request.
