1. Purpose

This document defines how Cloudax Ltd. identifies, analyses, evaluates, and treats AI-related risks across all AI systems within the AIMS scope. It implements Clause 6.1 of ISO/IEC 42001:2023 and supports the AI Risk Treatment Plan (AIMS-RTP-001) and the AI Risk Register (AI-REG-RISK-001).

ISO 27001:2022 references: ISO 42001 reference: Clause 6.1.1 General; Clause 6.1.2 AI risk assessment; Clause 6.1.3 AI risk treatment

2. Scope

This Methodology applies to all AI risks within the AIMS scope as defined in AIMS-SCOPE-001. It addresses risks to Cloudax, to its customers, to end-users, and to society arising from AI systems Cloudax develops, operates, supplies, or uses.

3. Definitions

  • AI risk: an effect of uncertainty on AI objectives, including AI-specific risks (bias, opacity, autonomy, emergent behaviour, drift) and risks where AI is a contributing factor.
  • Likelihood: the assessed probability of a risk materialising within a defined period.
  • Impact: the assessed severity of consequences if the risk materialises, considering effects on Cloudax, customers, end-users, and society.

4. Principles

  • AI risk is broader than information security risk: it includes ethical, fairness, transparency, and societal dimensions.
  • Risk assessment is forward-looking and evidence-based, drawing on operational data, threat intelligence, and ethical analysis.
  • Risk is owned by named individuals; risk is not a register entry without an owner.
  • Risk treatment is proportionate to risk — high-impact systems receive more rigorous treatment.
  • Risk is reassessed at defined triggers, not just on a calendar.

5. Roles and Responsibilities

  • AI Governance Lead: owns this Methodology, the Risk Register, and risk reporting; coordinates risk assessment activity.
  • Chief Technology Officer: contributes technical risk identification and treatment options.
  • Privacy Lead: contributes risks related to personal data and data subject impact.
  • Risk owners: assigned to each risk; responsible for tracking treatment and reporting status.
  • Chief Executive Officer: accepts critical residual risk; receives risk reports.

6. AI Risk Categories

AI risks are categorised to ensure systematic coverage:

  • Performance and accuracy: model errors, drift, edge-case failures, accuracy degradation over time.
  • Bias and fairness: systematic disparate treatment of protected groups; demographic skew in training data; biased outputs.
  • Privacy and data protection: training-data leakage; re-identification of pseudonymised data; data subject rights infringement.
  • Security: model theft; adversarial inputs; prompt injection; jailbreaking; supply-chain risk in AI components.
  • Transparency and explainability: inability to explain AI decisions; opacity to users; misleading communication of AI capability.
  • Autonomy and oversight: insufficient human oversight; over-automation of high-stakes decisions.
  • Lawful and ethical: regulatory non-compliance; ethical breach; reputational harm.
  • Societal and environmental: aggregate effects on employment, equity, environment.
  • Operational: incident response readiness; operational continuity of AI services.
  • Third-party: dependency, behavioural change, exit risk associated with third-party AI providers.

7. The Risk Assessment Process

7.1 Risk Identification

Risks are identified through:

  • Workshops with engineering and product personnel.
  • AISIA outputs (each AISIA may surface new risks).
  • Threat intelligence and incident reports (Cloudax and external).
  • Annex C of ISO/IEC 42001:2023 (Section 12 below).
  • Customer feedback and complaints.
  • Regulatory developments.

7.2 Risk Analysis (Likelihood and Impact)

Each identified risk is analysed for likelihood and impact using the scales in Sections 8 and 9. Analysis considers existing controls and assumes those controls are operating as designed (inherent risk) and as actually performing (residual risk).

7.3 Risk Evaluation

The risk score (likelihood × impact) is mapped to the matrix in Section 10, producing a risk band of Low, Moderate, High, or Critical. The band drives the treatment requirement and the acceptance authority (Section 11).

7.4 Risk Treatment

For each evaluated risk, treatment options are considered: avoid, modify (reduce likelihood or impact through controls), share (transfer or insure), or retain (accept). Treatment is recorded in AIMS-RTP-001.

12. AI-Specific Risk Sources (Annex C reference)

Annex C of ISO/IEC 42001:2023 catalogues AI-specific risk sources. Cloudax’s assessment considers, at minimum:

  • Complexity of the AI environment, including foundation model dependencies.
  • Lack of transparency / explainability of outputs.
  • Level of automation and reduction of human oversight.
  • Risk sources internal (data quality, model design choices, deployment context) and external (adversarial inputs, regulatory change, third-party behaviour).
  • Continuous learning and post-deployment behaviour change.
  • Hardware issues (e.g. specialised AI accelerator dependency, sustainability).
  • Life cycle and stage transitions (development → production → evolution → retirement).
  • Technology readiness and maturity.
  • Access to AI expertise.

13. Reassessment Triggers

  • Annual full risk assessment.
  • Material change to AI systems, third-party providers, or the regulatory environment.
  • AI incident with risk register implications.
  • AISIA identifying new risks.
  • Customer or regulator feedback indicating risk.

14. Records

  • The AI Risk Register (AI-REG-RISK-001) holds all identified risks with their analysis, evaluation, treatment, and review history.
  • The AI Risk Treatment Plan (AIMS-RTP-001) holds the planned treatment for each risk above the acceptance threshold.
  • Records are retained per POL-009.

15. Maintenance

This Methodology is reviewed at least annually. Material changes are approved by the Chief Executive Officer and recorded in Annex B.
